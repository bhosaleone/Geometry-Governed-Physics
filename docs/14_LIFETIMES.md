# Lecture 14: The Clock of Decay

Why do some particles last forever, while others vanish in a trillionth of a second? In the Standard Model, we use "coupling constants" and "phase space." In Geometry-Governed Physics, we use **Information Debt.**

## 1. The Stability Theorem
A particle is stable if its existence is "mathematically self-closing." This happens when its information debt ($n$) is a perfect integer.

- **The Electron ($n=0$)**: Zero debt. Perfectly stable.
- **The Proton ($n=4$)**: Integer debt. Stable (Theorem 3.1).

## 2. The Muon's Dilemma ($n=2.375$)
The Muon is a "flawed" version of the electron. Its information debt is fractional:
$$n_{eff} = \frac{19}{8} = 2.375$$

Because $0.375$ is leftover, the geometric closure isn't perfect. The particle "leaks" information into the vacuum. Eventually, the leak becomes so great that the particle's structural integrity fails. We call this **Decay.**

## 3. The Decay Formula
The rate of decay ($\Gamma$) is determined by the ratio of the **Bekenstein Limit** ($n_{max}$) to the particle's **Debt** ($n_{eff}$):

$$\Gamma = \omega_0 \cdot \exp\left( -\frac{n_{max}}{n_{eff}} \right)$$

- **$\omega_0$**: The "Geometric Clock" (approx $7.1 \times 10^{26}$ Hz).
- **$n_{max}$**: The maximum debt a particle can carry (approx 10,820).

## 4. Why the Proton Wins
For the Proton, $n=4$. But why doesn't it decay? 
According to **Theorem 3.1**, the Proton occupies the "Deepest Stable Mode" of the $S_5$ Kernel. To decay, it would need to move to a lower eigenvalue. But there *are* no lower eigenvalues that satisfy the conservation of the Baryon-Irrep. 

The Proton is stable not just because its debt is an integer, but because it has **nowhere lower to go.**
